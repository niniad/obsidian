## What are tantrums?

Tantrums are emotional outbursts. They are a normal part of a child‚Äôs emotional development. Your toddler expresses their anger, frustration, and other feelings through tantrums. In many cases, their outbursts can feel like a dramatic reaction to something very minor, like giving them a blue cup instead of a green one.

During a tantrum (also called a meltdown), your toddler may cry, scream, flail, bang their head, stomp their feet, or lie on the floor‚Äîthey‚Äôre out of control, emotionally and physically.

## Why do toddlers have tantrums?

As toddlers become more independent, they often don‚Äôt have the skills, language, or control to do or say what they want. When your toddler is trying to express what they want and feel, their language limitations can be extremely frustrating for them.

If your child is tired, hungry, sick, or off their normal routine, tantrums may be worse or happen more frequently.

## Can you stop a tantrum?

Once your toddler is overwhelmed by the emotion of a tantrum, most words won‚Äôt get through to them. The tantrum is a stress response, and the part of the brain called the amygdala is fully engaged, just as if your toddler were in a situation of real danger. Adults can sometimes use logic to reason themselves out of those feelings, but your toddler can‚Äôt. That‚Äôs why giving them explanations‚Äî‚ÄúBut the apple tastes just the same!‚Äù‚Äîwon‚Äôt help in the moment.

Instead, describe what you see, empathize, and be present: ‚ÄúYou‚Äôre really angry about that. I understand why you‚Äôre angry, and I‚Äôm right here if you need a hug.‚Äù This may or may not feel like it‚Äôs doing much, but it lets them know you hear them and understand how they feel.

## How long do tantrums last?

Your toddler‚Äôs tantrums may feel like they last forever, but most end within 10 minutes. You may feel overwhelmed in the moment, but remembering that it will be over soon could help you keep your cool and wait it out.

## When do toddlers stop having tantrums?

Research shows that a toddler‚Äôs tantrums typically happen less often and are less intense as their vocabulary grows. Your toddler will also have fewer tantrums as their abilities to manage disappointment and distress grow. Both of these skills develop over time, through the maturation of the frontal lobe and through the experience of [co-regulating with you](https://blog.lovevery.com/child-development/5-co-regulation-tips-to-help-your-toddler-manage-their-feelings/).

## 5 ways to deal with a tantrum

Remaining calm is one of the best‚Äîand yet one of the toughest‚Äîways to help your toddler settle down when they‚Äôre upset, says Felman. During a tantrum, your child needs to know that their emotions are manageable and that they‚Äôre safe. Try these strategies from Felman the next time your toddler melts down.

### 1. Stay close

Even if your toddler pushes you away, stay nearby. Remind them in simple terms that you understand that they‚Äôre upset. If you can, get down to their eye level or sit down next to them. This shows them that their emotions are important to you.

### 2. Resist trying to reason with them

When your toddler is in the throes of a tantrum, they can‚Äôt control their emotions or their body. Hearing that their tantrum behavior is wrong or trying to reason with them will likely just make them more upset.

### 3. Model empathy and calm

In an empathetic tone, validate their feelings: ‚ÄúYou are *really* sad that your balloon popped.‚Äù One of the best ways to teach your toddler emotional regulation is by example. If you can remain calm, over time, your toddler will be able to settle themselves more quickly.

### 4. Stop your toddler from hitting or biting

[If your toddler hits, kicks, or bites you during a tantrum](https://blog.lovevery.com/child-development/kicking-biting-and-hitting-understanding-and-responding-to-your-toddlers-tantrums/), you should address it clearly but with empathy. Try a strategy called **‚Äúconnection, then correction.‚Äù** This may be as simple as putting a hand over theirs and saying, ‚ÄúI can see that you‚Äôre upset.‚Äù Then, make it clear that their behavior isn‚Äôt safe: ‚ÄúHitting isn‚Äôt okay.‚Äù All emotions are okay, but some behaviors aren‚Äôt.

It can be tempting to talk through the tantrum right after it happens. But once your toddler has calmed down and they‚Äôre starting to move on, move on with them. Reconnect by holding hands, singing a song, or just sharing a big hug.

Adults tend to hang onto hard moments longer than toddlers do. Remember, a tantrum at this age isn‚Äôt a teachable moment‚Äîthe best thing to do is to continue on as if it never happened. This helps your toddler learn that they‚Äôre going to have big, overwhelming emotions, and those feelings will come and go.

## Should you ignore your toddler during a tantrum?

‚ÄúIgnore the behavior‚Äù is common advice for handling tantrums, but that doesn‚Äôt mean you should ignore your child when they‚Äôre having a hard time. Ignoring their behavior means continuing to stay physically and emotionally present without commenting on the flailing, screaming, and crying. During a tantrum, your toddler is overwhelmed by their emotions and needs your love and support to get through it.

## How to survive a public meltdown

If tantrums at home are hard, the ones that happen out in public can be even more stressful. When your toddler screams and cries in the grocery store, it‚Äôs easy to feel really embarrassed or self conscious. Suddenly, your parenting is on display, and that could make you question yourself or respond in a way that you may not at home.

* **Move your toddler to a different location, if you can.** A change of scenery is often the best remedy for a toddler wrestling with big feelings. * **If you can‚Äôt move your toddler to a more private spot‚Äîsay, you‚Äôre on a plane and the seatbelt sign is on‚Äîyou‚Äôll have to co-regulate them through their tantrum as best you can**. It‚Äôs so hard when you feel people watching you, but try to tune them out. Your toddler will recover more quickly if you remain a calm, stable, and loving presence. In the end, what matters most is your relationship with your child.

## How to keep calm during your toddler‚Äôs tantrums

**Take some deep breaths**. We know you hear it all the time, but that‚Äôs because research shows it works. Breathe in through your nose for a count of five, hold it for five counts, then exhale for a count of five.

**Sit or lie down near your toddler**. Tell them, ‚ÄúI need to lie down, but I‚Äôm right here next to you.‚Äù

**Keep talking to a minimum**. Chatter can overwhelm your toddler during a tantrum, and talking less can help you stay grounded and present.
