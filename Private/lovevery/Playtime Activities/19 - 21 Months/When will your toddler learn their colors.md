## A timeline of color understanding

**12 to 18 months: start to notice that objects come in different colors.** One-year-olds may show a preference for certain colors. For instance, your toddler may specifically choose a green ball from a basket full of colored balls.

**18 to 24 months: begin to learn color words.** When asked ‚ÄúWhat color is it?‚Äù your child may respond with a color term, though it may not be the correct one. For example, they may hold a yellow ball that and say ‚Äúblue.‚Äù Even though your toddler probably can‚Äôt label a color correctly, their intuitive understanding of colors is developing. **24 to 36 months: correctly identify a few colors.** Children can show that they know the correct names for a few colors when asked. Your toddler may point to the right cup if asked, ‚ÄúWhere is the red cup?‚Äù¬†This shows they can understand color words (receptive language) before they can accurately come up with them on their own (productive language).

**After 36 months: come to understand that color is a distinct quality.** Children learn to separate the attribute of color from the object itself. Objects can have different colors, even if their function is the same. This is when children can name colors with more accuracy.

## 5 fun ways to help your toddler learn colors

As you do these activities, try [saying the color name after the object name](https://blog.lovevery.com/child-development/the-better-way-to-help-your-toddler-learn-colors/). Research shows this can be an effective way to help young children learn color words. For example, you can say, ‚ÄúThe ball is purple‚Äù instead of ‚ÄúThis is a purple ball.‚Äù

### 1. Create a color box

Take a small box or bin and fill it with objects that are all the same color, like yellow‚Äîfor example, a lemon, a cup, a ball, and some playthings: the star and basket from the [Transfer Tweezers & Felt Stars](https://lovevery.com/products/the-play-kits-the-companion), the piece from the [Chunky Wooden Puzzle](https://lovevery.com/products/the-play-kits-the-companion), and a peg from the [Wooden Peg Stand](https://lovevery.com/products/the-play-kits-the-companion). Place the bin on the floor so your toddler can explore on their own at first. After a few minutes, talk to them about the objects and their colors: ‚ÄúThis star is yellow. Will the star fit in the yellow cup?‚Äù

### 2. Taste a color

At snack time, provide a plate of foods that are all the same color: green kiwi, green edamame, green snap pea crisps, green cucumber. If your toddler enjoys their green snacks, try working your way through the rainbow with a different color snack each day.

### 3. Compare colors at the store

As you go about your daily routine, name objects and their colors. This is especially fun at a grocery store, where lots of different colors are on display: ‚ÄúThis apple is red and that pepper is also red. Two foods that are red!‚Äù

### 4. Read a colorful book

Pick a book with realistic photos, like ‚ÄúColorful Foods‚Äù ([from The Babbler Play Kit Book Bundle](https://blog.lovevery.com/product-recommendations/introducing-book-bundles-for-the-play-kits-by-lovevery/)). Point to the objects and name the colors as you read together: ‚ÄúThat boy is eating a strawberry. The strawberry is red. The boy‚Äôs shirt is also red.‚Äù

### 5. Go on a color hunt

Take your toddler on an outdoor color scavenger hunt. As you explore nature, work together to find different colored objects. You can take the lead in discovering all the green objects at the park and pointing them out to your child.
