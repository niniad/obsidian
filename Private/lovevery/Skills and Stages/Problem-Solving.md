## **What is problem-solving?**

Problem-solving is the process by which your child spots a problem and comes up with a solution to overcome it. Your child uses problem-solving skills in all sorts of contexts, from figuring out how to get a ball out of a cup to interacting with a child who took their toy.

Children don‚Äôt inherently understand different approaches to solving problems‚Äîthese skills develop gradually over time, starting in the earliest days of life. As your child gains experience, tests out strategies, plays with various materials, and watches people around them, they learn how to problem-solve.

## **What are examples of problem-solving skills?**

Think about strategies you might use to tackle a project at work‚Äîfor example, creating an outline, breaking the project into steps, or delegating tasks. With your help, your child will develop problem-solving skills like these:

* Breaking a large problem into **smaller steps**
* **Persevering** through challenges or setbacks
* Using **creativity** to think ‚Äúoutside the box‚Äù about different solutions
* Being **resourceful** by using available items as tools to reach a goal
* Taking the **initiative** to try a possible solution and see if it works
* Seeking **help** when you get stuck
* Using **compromise** or negotiation to help resolve a conflict
* Using **critical thinking** to discover what the next step should be

## **When do children develop problem-solving skills?**

As early as 8 to 11 months, you may see the earliest signs of your child‚Äôs problem-solving skills at work. If you hide a toy under a blanket or basket, for example, they may use basic problem-solving to try to uncover it.

As a toddler, your child will grow more experienced with different types of playthings and the challenges they offer. They‚Äôll also [develop more focus and patience](https://blog.lovevery.com/child-development/subtle-signs-of-your-toddlers-developing-focus/) to work through problems on their own. Support their emerging problem-solving skills by observing their efforts‚Äîwithout stepping in right away to help. It‚Äôs tempting to intervene when you see your toddler struggle to fit the pieces of a puzzle, align blocks so they won‚Äôt fall, or get a stuck car out of the [Race & Chase Ramp](https://lovevery.com/products/the-play-kits-the-adventurer). Banging, rotating, failing, and trying again are all important parts of the process. Your toddler gains more problem-solving experience with every attempt.

**RELATED:** [**Subtle signs of your toddler‚Äôs developing focus**](https://blog.lovevery.com/child-development/subtle-signs-of-your-toddlers-developing-focus/)

By 3 years of age, your child will have more skills to help them solve a problem. They‚Äôve learned how to communicate and follow directions. They also have more control over their emotions and their body. Not only are they ready to solve more complex puzzles and games, they‚Äôre¬† learning how to solve social problems, like working through conflict and negotiating with peers during play.

## **Why are problem-solving skills important in child development?**

If your child is accustomed to tackling problems, they‚Äôre more likely to at least attempt to get the cup they need off the high shelf, or try to buckle those tricky sandal straps. Practicing problem-solving can help your child overcome challenges, try flexible ways of thinking, and become more confident and independent in the process.

Problem-solving skills are also crucial to your child‚Äôs cognitive development. They encourage your child‚Äôs brain to make new connections and process information in new ways. This is why so many of the best games, toys, and activities for young children stress some element of problem-solving, critical thinking, or creativity.

Your child can develop better social skills when they practice problem-solving, too: Understanding how to resolve conflicts and compromise with peers is a crucial problem-solving skill they‚Äôll take with them into preschool and beyond.

## **Stacking toys**

Stacking toys such as blocks or rings engage babies and toddlers in a challenging form of problem-solving play. Your child‚Äôs skills are put to the test as they plan where to place each item, work to balance their stack, and wrestle with gravity to keep the stack from toppling.

You can introduce your baby to stacking play around 9 to 10 months with playthings that are easy to work with, like the [Nesting Stacking Drip Drop Cups](https://lovevery.com/products/the-play-kits-the-inspector). Stacking takes coordination, precision, and patience, and if they try to stack items that are too difficult to keep upright, they may become frustrated and give up.

You can also make basic blocks easier to stack by using a larger item as a base. Demonstrate how to stack a block on top of the base, then knock the tower down. Hand a block to your toddler and allow them to try stacking and knocking it down. As their movements become more controlled and purposeful, introduce another block to stack.

Stacking a tower with the pegs from the [Wooden Stacking Pegboard](https://lovevery.com/products/the-play-kits-the-adventurer) is a fun way to introduce goal-setting, an important aspect of problem-solving. The pegs nest together securely, allowing your toddler to build a higher, more stable tower than they could create with regular blocks. You can gently suggest a goal for your child‚Äî‚ÄúCan we stack it higher?‚Äù‚Äîand see if they‚Äôre ready for the challenge. Then, sit and support them as they try to solve any problems that arise: ‚ÄúIs the tower too tall? Can we make it wider so it won‚Äôt fall so easily?‚Äù

## **Pretend play**

Pretend play supports your child‚Äôs problem-solving skills in many ways. Research suggests that children‚Äôs pretend play is linked to different types of problem-solving and creativity. For example, one study showed that pretend play with peers was linked to better divergent problem-solving‚Äîmeaning that children were able to ‚Äúthink outside the box‚Äù to solve problems.

Pretend play is also a safe place for children to recreate‚Äîand practice solving‚Äîproblems they‚Äôve seen in their lives. A 3- or 4-year-old child might be ready to explore creative problem-solving through pretend play that uses their playthings in new ways. Help your child start with an idea: ‚ÄúWhat do you want to pretend to be or recreate ‚Äî a favorite storybook scene or someone from real life like a doctor or server at a restaurant?‚Äù Then encourage them to look for playthings they can use to pretend. Research finds that parents who model ‚Äúout of the box‚Äù ways to play can encourage creative thinking and problem-solving in their children, starting in toddlerhood.

## **Problem-solving and frustration tolerance**

It can be difficult for young children to manage their frustration, but giving your child opportunities to solve problems on their own helps build both confidence and [frustration tolerance](https://blog.lovevery.com/child-development/11-ways-to-build-your-toddlers-frustration-tolerance/). Research suggests that the ability to set goals and persist in them through challenges‚Äîsometimes called ‚Äúgrit‚Äù‚Äîis linked to school and career success. Here‚Äôs how you can play an important role in helping your child develop problem-solving persistence.

**Model persistence.** You know your toddler closely observes everything you do üôÉ A 2017 study shows that young children who watch their parents persist in their own challenge were more likely to show persistence themselves. Allow your toddler to see you attempting an activity, failing, and talking yourself through trying again. While playing with blocks, try stacking a few off balance so they fall. Notice aloud what went wrong and continue to narrate as you move slowly to carefully stack the blocks again.

**Give them time.** A little frustration can go a long way toward learning. It can take enormous restraint not to point out where to put the puzzle piece or how to slot the peg in place‚Äîbut try to give them time to problem-solve on their own. You‚Äôre helping them feel capable and confident when faced with new challenges.

**Ask questions to encourage new strategies.** If your toddler gets frustrated with a problem, encourage their problem-solving process by asking questions: ‚ÄúAre you trying to race the car down the ramp but it got stuck? Is the car too long to go down sideways?‚Äù This may help your child refocus their attention on their goal instead of what they have already unsuccessfully tried. With a little time and creative problem-solving, your child may figure it out on their own.

## **Developmental concerns with problem-solving**

Problem-solving skills are just one component of your child‚Äôs overall cognitive development. By around 12 months of age, you should see signs that your child is *attempting* to solve simple problems, like looking for a toy under a blanket. By about 30 months, your child may show slightly more advanced problem-solving skills, like using a stool to reach a high counter. Their attempts might not always be successful at this age, but the fact that they‚Äôre trying shows they‚Äôre thinking through different options. If you don‚Äôt see signs of your child trying to solve problems in these ways, talk to your pediatrician about your concerns. They can assess your child‚Äôs overall development and answer any questions.
