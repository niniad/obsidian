## How to hold your newborn

For the first few weeks of a baby‚Äôs life, it‚Äôs important to support their head and neck whenever you pick them up or hold them. When lifting your baby, slide one of your hands under their head and neck as you place your other hand under their bottom. Continue providing this support as long as you hold them. You can also cradle their head in the crook of your bent arm.  
  
During these early weeks, you‚Äôll want to routinely and gently [reposition your newborn‚Äôs head](https://blog.lovevery.com/child-development/head-turning-dont-miss-this-expert-mamas-essential-newborn-tip/). This stretches the muscles on both sides of their neck to promote flexibility and help prevent Flat Head Syndrome, or plagiocephaly, a condition in which a flat spot develops.

Until your baby can hold their head steady in an upright position, be sure to only use babywearing devices that provide head support. When your baby is in the carrier, keep them positioned with their belly or side against your body and their chin away from their chest to keep their airway open. Once your baby has dynamic head control‚Äîmeaning they can hold their head steady, even when you move, bend, stop, or turn quickly‚Äîthey can safely face forward in a carrier, with their back against your body.

## When can babies hold their head up?

Babies develop head control gradually. They typically start to direct their head movements in their third month and gain more control between 4 and 5 months of age. Most have full head control by the time they‚Äôre 6 months old.

When a baby is placed on their back just after birth, their head will turn to one side or the other. Within the first two months of life, they‚Äôll likely gain enough neck strength and muscle coordination to both hold their head steady and centered and turn it fully to each side as they lay on their back. In tummy time, they may briefly lift their head slightly and unsteadily‚Äîturned to one side or the other‚Äîat first, and then begin turning it to the opposite side occasionally. When you hold them upright against your body, they may hold their head up momentarily with a lot of wobbling‚Äîand your hand poised to catch it if it falls back.

By 3 or 4 months old, most babies develop the strength and endurance to hold their head up and keep it centered with more steadiness and for longer periods. At this stage, your baby may begin to turn their head toward the sound of a voice.

At 4 to 6 months old, your baby may lift their head high during tummy time and turn it fully to each side with control. Between 5 and 7 months, most babies use intentional head turns when lying down to help them roll from their back to their belly and from their belly to their back.

The final stage is dynamic head control‚Äîthe ability to keep their head upright even when their body‚Äôs position changes. Your baby has gained this level of head control when they can hold their head steady as you lift them from a reclined position. Dynamic head control is essential for your baby to sit up on their own.

## How to help your baby develop head control

Giving your baby plenty of floor time on their back, tummy, and each side is the best way to support their effort to gain head control. This helps build the neck and back muscles needed to lift and control their head. Try these tips:

* Place your baby on their back in [The Play Gym](https://lovevery.com/products/the-play-gym), then use visually interesting playthings and sounds to encourage your baby to turn their head as they.
* Lay your baby on your chest, your lap, or the floor to practice tummy time. Hold a high-contrast toy or image directly in front of them, so they try to lift their head.
* With your baby on your lap, momentarily support them at their chest or shoulders to let them practice holding up their head. As they start to develop more strength, you can try supporting them at their waist.
* Hold your baby upright on your shoulder, so they can gaze out a window.

## Head control challenges

Most babies develop stable head control by around 5 to 6 months of age. If you have any concerns about your baby‚Äôs ability to hold up or move their head, reach out to their pediatrician. Here are a few scenarios that call for an assessment:

* At 2 months, your baby is unable to briefly lift their cheek off the floor during tummy time. Tight muscles on one side of the neck, known as torticollis, causes a baby‚Äôs head to consistently turn or tilt in one direction. It can happen for a variety of reasons, but often, it‚Äôs brought on by the baby‚Äôs position in the womb. Prompt treatment can prevent motor delays.
* Your baby has a distinct preference for turning their head to a particular side when lying on their back. Spending more time lying with their head facing in one direction increases the chance of developing Flat Head Syndrome.
* Your baby isn‚Äôt helping to hold their head upright by 4 months of age. While total head control isn‚Äôt expected yet, your baby‚Äôs head should be much less floppy than it was at 1 to 2 months of age.
