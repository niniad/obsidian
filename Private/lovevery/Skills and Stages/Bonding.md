## Why bonding is important for child development

Studies show children who receive focused, consistent attention and physical intimacy from a parent or primary caregiver have more emotional stability, higher IQ scores, and better physical health than children who don‚Äôt. In other words, what we know instinctively to be true‚Äîthat all babies need love‚Äîhas a lot of scientific evidence to back it up.

Early relationships shape the architecture of your baby‚Äôs brain, influence their self-esteem, and form a model for their future social attachments. Meeting your baby‚Äôs physical needs for food, sleep, and cleanliness is essential to forming a strong bond. But addressing their emotional needs for security, soothing, and comfort is important, too.

A strong, positive attachment to a caregiver serves as a buffer against stress, allowing a baby to confidently explore the world and recover more quickly from setbacks. At the same time, chronic or extreme forms of stress‚Äîlike poverty, abuse, exposure to violence, or neglect‚Äîcan harm brain development and long-term well-being in children.

## How babies recognize their parents

When babies are born, their vision is still developing. Your baby can‚Äôt see your face clearly yet, but they can recognize you by the sound of your voice or the smell of your skin. By 2 to 4 months of age, their eyesight has typically improved and they begin to recognize your face. Their acknowledgment of you and other regular caregivers will grow over time. In just a few months, they‚Äôll get visibly excited when they see you üôÇ

## How to bond with your baby

Contrary to what you may have heard, you really can‚Äôt spoil your baby by holding them too much. When your baby is content, you can set them down in a safe place within eyeshot for a few minutes, but they may not want to be out of your arms for long. Each time you respond to their needs and hold or talk to them, you help them feel safe and strengthen their trust in you.

Caring for your baby also helps build your connection to them. Renowned developmental psychologist Alison Gopnik explains the nature of the human bonding process: ‚ÄúEven more than other animals, humans respond to the act of caring itself. Taking care of a baby helps us love that baby, and that baby in particular. Holding and caring for babies increases our [levels of] oxytocin‚Äî[the hormone responsible for] that warm feeling inside.‚Äù

![](https://i0.wp.com/blog.lovevery.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/Lovevery-VKS-Lifestyle-APPSHOOT-Sep-10-2021-0068_BH_EDIT.jpg?resize=1024%2C607&ssl=1)

At the heart of bonding are touch and communication. Here are some bonding practices you can include in your baby‚Äôs daily routine:

* **Practice skin-to-skin contact.** Holding your baby naked against your skin can help you feel close to each other. The sound of your heartbeat and voice is also calming to them and can regulate their temperature and breathing.
* **Make eye contact, especially during feedings.** Communicating with your eyes and facial expressions is meaningful to your baby before they understand words. Make a point to meet your baby‚Äôs eyes while feeding them. Your baby may not actively look at you the whole time, but your attentiveness will reinforce your bond.
* **Discover and respond to your baby‚Äôs cues.** Before your baby can talk, you‚Äôll begin to pick up on their subtle signs that they‚Äôre hungry, tired, or in need of soothing. As you respond, your baby will begin to understand that their efforts to communicate have results. For example, if you turn on a bright light and your baby scrunches up their eyes, turns their head, or arches their back, you can stroke them and say, ‚ÄúOh, that light is so bright. I‚Äôll turn it off.‚Äù After you switch it off, you can say, ‚ÄúThere. Is that better?‚Äù
* **Have ‚Äúconversations.‚Äù** Your baby will use body language, eye contact, cries, and coos to show they want to interact with you. When they do, be sure to reply. For example, if they look at you and coo, you might say, ‚ÄúYes, I hear you. Are you saying hi?‚Äù Your baby may coo again in response. This back-and-forth interaction is essential to both bonding and their brain development.

### Bonding activities for 0 to 3 months

**Face-to-face focusing practice: 0 to 2 months**

Interacting with your baby as they rest on your body facing you can boost their emotional and cognitive development. They learn by looking at your face, while being in close physical contact with you helps them feel calm.

Insert the [Simple Black & White Cards](https://lovevery.com/products/the-play-kits-the-looker) in the [Standing Card Holder](https://lovevery.com/products/the-play-kits-the-looker). Sit on the floor with your knees bent and feet flat on the floor and prop your baby‚Äôs back against your thighs. Hold the card holder in front of your chest, so your baby can see the high-contrast patterns. This encourages them to practice focusing their eyes to help improve their blurry vision. As your baby examines the images, join in by smiling and talking about them in an animated, high-pitched voice.

**Book sharing: 1 to 2 months**

The goal of exploring books with your newborn is to bond over a shared experience. Read the words or describe the images, but you don‚Äôt need to get through every page. Focus instead on tuning in and connecting with your baby. Try these strategies with the Wooden Book:

* Make eye contact and talk slowly as you describe what‚Äôs on the page.
* Pause and give your baby time to respond with a smile or coo.
* Watch for signs that your baby‚Äôs interested‚Äîfor example, they may focus on an image or kick their legs.
* When your baby starts to look away or make fewer sounds or movements, it‚Äôs time to stop.

**Faces in the mirror: 2 to 3 months**

Your baby is fascinated with faces right now‚Äîespecially yours ‚ù§Ô∏è They learn by studying your expressions, your eyes, and your mouth movements. At this age, face-to-face play can help your baby develop their attention span, understand social cues, and deepen their connection to you.

Attach the [Mirror Card](https://lovevery.com/products/the-play-gym) to the clips on [The Play Gym](https://lovevery.com/products/the-play-gym). Lie next to your baby with the mirror in view and talk about what they see. This builds their visual skills, social-emotional awareness, and bond with you.

### Bonding activities for 3 to 6 months

**New twists on skin to skin: 4 months**

Skin-on-skin isn‚Äôt just for newborns. It offers sensory stimulation that encourages bonding, prompt social and cognitive development, and reduces crying long after the early weeks. Continue offering it during feedings and at other convenient opportunities. Try taking a bath with your baby or giving them a gentle massage with their skin on yours.

**Magic Tissue turns: 5 to 6 months**

Practicing shared attention with your baby can boost their focus and help you tune into each other‚Äôs nonverbal cues. To do this, try taking turns pulling tissues out of the [Magic Tissue Box](https://lovevery.com/products/the-play-kits-the-senser) with your baby.

Start by modeling the action, then encourage your baby to imitate you. Narrate as you go: ‚ÄúIt‚Äôs your turn to pull. Look, it‚Äôs a yellow tissue. Can you grab the tissue? You did it!‚Äù ‚ù§Ô∏è

### Bonding activities for 6 to 9 months

**Family picture book browsing: 7 to 8 months**

Your baby may be starting to recognize photos of the special people in their life. Try turning the [Soft Book](https://lovevery.com/products/the-play-kits-the-charmer) into a family photo album to reinforce their connection to these loved ones.

Slide images of family members into the book‚Äôs insert windows. Let your baby page through the book independently or sit them on your lap and look at it together. Tell stories about people in the photos and describe your baby‚Äôs relationship to them. For example, you could say, ‚ÄúLook, there‚Äôs Papa. Remember when he took you to the playground?‚Äù

**Serve-and-return exchanges: 6 to 7 months**

Serve and return is a phrase coined by Harvard researchers to describe back-and-forth social interactions. One person ‚Äúserves‚Äù by offering a sound, smile, word, or gesture, and the other ‚Äúreturns‚Äù with one of their own. Noticing and returning your baby‚Äôs ‚Äúserves‚Äù is a great way to practice:

* When your baby looks at you, smile at them.
* When they wave their arms, say, ‚ÄúHi, Baby!‚Äù
* When your baby makes a sound, repeat that sound back.

Interactions like these teach your baby the pattern of conversation, strengthen your emotional bond, and contribute to their cognitive growth. It may not always be easy to tell when they‚Äôre seeking an exchange with you, but even if you‚Äôre unsure, you can take the opportunity to connect by talking to them ‚ù§Ô∏è

### Bonding activities for 9 to 12 months

**Joint discoveries in nature: 9 to 12 months**

Taking your baby outdoors to introduce them to elements of the natural world encourages both of you to tune into each other‚Äôs actions and reactions. Let your baby examine grass and leaves up close. Find a tree and encourage them to touch the trunk as you describe its texture‚Äîrough, bumpy, or smooth. Sharing explorations like this strengthens the connection between the two of you.

**A diaper change connection: 11 to 12 months**

At around this age, your baby may struggle to lie down long enough for a diaper change. The [‚ÄúAnimals I See‚Äù Mini Book](https://lovevery.com/products/the-play-kits-the-thinker) may engage your baby long enough for you to change their diaper and have a social exchange with them about the book.

## When a baby prefers one parent

Starting at 2 to 4 months, your baby may show a clear preference for one parent or caregiver. They can identify close caregivers by smell, sight, and sound, and may want to be with the person who takes care of them most often.

This is very common but can be hard for everyone involved. If you‚Äôre the preferred parent, you may feel overwhelmed while your partner feels helpless and unable to offer comfort and support. No matter who your baby prefers or how long this phase lasts, they will ultimately form a healthy bond with all the loving adults in their life ‚ù§Ô∏è

These tips may be helpful if your baby begins to express a caregiver preference:

**Remember that it isn‚Äôt personal.** If your baby cries when you hold them and is only soothed when you return them to your partner, it can feel like a slight. But your baby doesn‚Äôt mean anything by it‚Äîthey don‚Äôt have any sense of the impact their preference can have on others.

**Give your baby a chance to warm up.** If you‚Äôre away all day, you may want to pick up your baby as soon as you get home. Try to take a moment to read their cues. If they‚Äôre smiling and looking at you, they‚Äôre likely ready to be held. If they look away or don‚Äôt smile, give them some time. Sit near them and softly talk to them for a while before holding them.

**Schedule one-on-one time with both parents.** Planning for the nonpreferred parent to handle a routine, like bath time, tends to work well. During this time, it‚Äôs best if the preferred caregiver stays out of sight and far enough away that their scent isn‚Äôt detectable to the baby‚Äôs strong sense of smell.

**Let your baby experience some disappointment.** It‚Äôs okay for them to feel sad about not being with a preferred caregiver. Healthy attachment involves your baby learning that multiple people can soothe them, and that people who leave will come back. If your baby won‚Äôt take a bottle from you, for example, there can be some logistical challenges. But it doesn‚Äôt mean anything is wrong. It‚Äôs just not something you do together right now.

**Recognize that this is a phase.** It may take days, weeks, or months, but at some point, your baby‚Äôs strong preference for one caregiver will soften or pass. Some babies may cycle between preferring one caregiver, then the other, and typical developments like separation and stranger anxieties can prompt changes in your baby‚Äôs preferences. This kind of partiality is a typical part of a baby‚Äôs social-emotional development and often ebbs and flows.

## Bonding challenges

It can take a little while for a parent to feel emotionally connected to their new baby. Expect your relationship to blossom gradually as you and your baby get to know each other.

Newborn care can feel overwhelming. In the early months, your baby‚Äôs needs are constant, and your sleep may be frequently interrupted. This can make being emotionally available to bond with your baby more difficult.

For biological mothers, the hormone shifts that follow childbirth can add to the challenge. These cause up to 80% of mothers to experience the baby blues‚Äîmood swings, tearfulness, and feelings of anxiety or restlessness. Fortunately, the rollercoaster of emotions is usually short-lived. It typically begins within the first few days after delivery, peaks at around one week, and resolves by the end of the second week postpartum without any intervention. If you have similar symptoms that begin, persist, or worsen more than two weeks after your baby‚Äôs delivery, reach out to your doctor for support.

Fathers and adoptive parents also experience hormone changes associated with caregiving, sleep deprivation, and new roles and responsibilities. If you have ongoing feelings of anxiety, sadness, or emptiness, see your healthcare provider.
