### Build a tower together

One of the simplest ways to practice turn-taking is with a set of blocks. By age 3, many children can build a tower up to nine or ten blocks high. Taking turns with a partner is a fun way to work toward a common goal.

If you can, start the tower on a hard, level surface‚Äîcarpets are tricky for balancing blocks.

Be clear with your language and offer your child a pattern to remember: ‚ÄúWe‚Äôre going to take turns building a tower together, as high as we can! We place them on the tower one block at a time, like this: First me, then you, then me, then you, until it falls over.‚Äù

Try to resist the urge to straighten or fix your child‚Äôs block placement. They learn a lot about balance each time the tower topples.

If they try to place two blocks on the same turn, you can gently remind them: ‚ÄúWait, it‚Äôs my turn! Remember the pattern?‚Äù

When your child does this with a peer, you may need to moderate more closely‚Äîthough if they get into a good rhythm taking turns, you can always take a step back.

### DIY bowling alley

Bowling is an easy game to adapt for young children and a great opportunity to practice turn-taking. A DIY bowling alley can be assembled with only a handful of everyday materials. Here‚Äôs how:

Collect a few empty bottles‚Äîplastic beverage bottles of any size work great. Wash them out and weigh them down with a little sand or water. Screw the lids on tight and find a ball that rolls well.

You can start with as few as three ‚Äúbowling pins‚Äù and go as high as 10‚Äîif you have more, your child will have an easier time knocking them over. Set up the bottles in a triangular pattern, with the point facing you, and sit or stand a few feet away.

Take turns rolling the ball toward the bottles until they‚Äôve all been knocked over.

Between each round, invite your child to help you pick up the bottles. Standing them upright is a motor precision challenge, and you can put dot stickers on the floor to indicate where the bottles should go.

### Turn-taking ball

Having a physical item to hold when it‚Äôs their turn to do something can help children more easily internalize the concept. A ball, especially a soft one they can squeeze, is a great tool‚Äîbut you can really use any soft object. Here are a few ways to use the turn-taking ball:

When you first introduce it, you can call the object the ‚Äúturn ball‚Äù and explain that only the person holding it can do something‚Äîtalk, jump, dance, smile‚Äîbut that their partner will get a turn really soon.

Practice in small bursts, not allowing 5 to 10 seconds to pass before your child gets the ‚Äúball.‚Äù They will likely need prompting to return it to you.
