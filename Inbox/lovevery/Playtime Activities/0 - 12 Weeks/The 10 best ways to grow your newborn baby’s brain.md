Some tips for developing your baby‚Äôs eyes:

* Start with simple images about 12 inches away from your baby‚Äôs face (about the distance from your wrist to your elbow). Hold the images steady and try not to switch them until your baby looks away, a sign that they‚Äôre losing interest. You may notice your baby staring at the images for many seconds, even minutes at a time.
* When they lose interest in an image, change to a new one and eventually switch to more¬†[complex images](https://lovevery.com/products/the-play-kits-the-looker)¬†as their eyes grow stronger.
* You can help promote visual tracking by slowly moving an image back and forth horizontally in front of their face to help them practice following a moving object with their eyes. This is an important skill later for reading, writing, and hand-eye coordination.
* Offer high-contrast images¬†in the car, during [tummy time](https://blog.lovevery.com/child-development/4-ideas-for-no-floor-less-fussy-tummy-time/), and during alert ‚Äúplay‚Äù times for the first 14 weeks.

### **Protection from multiple, jumbled noises**

A newborn‚Äôs nervous system truly doesn‚Äôt have a filter: They can‚Äôt screen out anything that their eyes see, their ears hear, or their skin feels. Several sound sources at once‚Äîeven a typical coffee shop atmosphere with music playing, several people talking, traffic noise, etc.‚Äîcan easily overstimulate and confuse a baby, causing them to shut down. Your newborn prefers the sound of one human voice at a time and calm,¬†[rhythmic tones](https://www.washington.edu/news/2016/04/25/music-improves-baby-brain-responses-to-music-and-speech/).

### **Help to stretch out from the womb position**

After all that time in the womb, it‚Äôs important for new babies to stretch out of the fetal position and use their muscles in new ways. Offering a variety of positions throughout the day can help your baby [avoid flat spots](https://blog.lovevery.com/child-development/the-facts-on-flat-head-syndrome/) on their head, help their brain start to understand where their body is positioned in space, and build strength. Baby carriers, car seats, baby swings, and strollers are amazing and very necessary at times, but try to give your baby some time on a [flat surface](https://lovevery.com/products/the-play-gym) every day.

Some tips to avoid flat spots and help your baby gain strength:

* Occupational therapists recommend¬†**turning your baby‚Äôs head from side to side**¬†to avoid a flat spot forming and neck muscles tightening.¬†[Here‚Äôs a video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Gs5jbSDuYTw)¬†from Rachel Coley, a Pediatric Occupational Therapist with advanced specialized training in infant neurodevelopment. Babies are generally born with a preference for turning their head to one side‚Äîmatching their position in the womb‚Äîso it‚Äôs important to help your baby learn to turn both ways.
* **Side lying**¬†is also good for your baby, and is usually less frustrating than tummy time can be. Lying on their side helps your baby build strength in their back and belly, and also encourages them to bring their hands together‚Äîan important developmental step.

Here‚Äôs how to do it:

* Roll up a blanket and use it to prop your baby on¬†their side, supporting¬†their back (always with your supervision).
* You can put [black and white cards in a standing card holder](https://lovevery.com/products/the-play-kits-the-looker) in your baby‚Äôs line of sight for something to engage them.
* Eventually, switch¬†to the other side‚Äîand repeat.
* Babies also enjoy lying on their backs with their heads to one side. Be sure to switch sides occasionally by gently turning¬†their head.

### **Low lights when awake**

After 9 months in the dim light of the womb, a newborn‚Äôs eyes take a few weeks to adjust to more intense light. In low light and when awake, newborns will open their eyes and look around the room for a few moments. This early looking helps them practice working both eyes together (it‚Äôs common for a newborn‚Äôs eyes not to look in the same direction) and also exercises their pupillary reflex, where the pupil gets bigger or smaller based on different amounts of light. You can help develop your baby‚Äôs [brain-eye connection](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2015/09/150929150631.htm) by gradually varying the intensity of the light (avoid strong lights, such as direct sun). You can use a dimmer switch, a low lamp, or curtains when your baby is alert.

### **Skin-on-skin time**

![baby and mom doing skin-to-skin time](https://i0.wp.com/blog.lovevery.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/07/7Z6A2390-Edit.jpg?resize=8192%2C4854&ssl=1)

[Skin-on-skin time](https://blog.lovevery.com/child-development/why-skin-to-skin-time-isnt-just-for-the-hospital/) can feel inconvenient sometimes, but it‚Äôs worth it. It offers a huge array of benefits to your baby: better digestion, temperature regulation, weight gain, improved immunity, improved sleep, and even brain growth. There are benefits for parents as well: feel-good hormones are released when you and your baby have direct skin contact.

Here are some tips and ideas for skin-on-skin:

* Feeding time is a great opportunity for skin-on-skin time. Strip your baby down to a diaper, take your shirt off, and use a blanket over your shoulders for warmth. Before you get undressed, try to remember to use the bathroom, get a glass of water, your phone, a book, or anything else you want handy in case your baby falls asleep and you want to relax together.
* Try taking a bath with your baby. Make sure to have towels ready on the floor within reach before you get in, and someone to help you and your baby get out of the bath if possible. A space heater in the bathroom can give you a little extra warmth‚Äîjust make sure it‚Äôs as far from the tub as possible.
* If your baby enjoys massage, your light touch is great for skin-on-skin contact. If you use baby oil or a natural oil like coconut, test a small area of your baby‚Äôs skin first to ensure there‚Äôs no allergic reaction. [Massage for babies](https://blog.lovevery.com/child-development/mindful-baby-massage/) is most calming from the center of their bodies out, so start with your baby‚Äôs chest and move out to legs and arms. Make sure the room is warm and use dark or older towels under and around your baby. Sometimes the massage oil can stain fresh, white towels.
* Skin-on-skin works great for dads, moms, and even supervised siblings. If you‚Äôre feeling down, worried, or anxious‚Äîand what new parent isn‚Äôt sometimes?‚Äîskin-on-skin contact will release oxytocin, a natural antidote. If they‚Äôre awake, this is also a great chance for tummy time.

### **The sound of your voice**

![Baby and woman looking at each other](https://i0.wp.com/blog.lovevery.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/2021_29_09_LOOKBOOK0227_BH_EDIT-e1635970128410.jpg?resize=7931%2C4699&ssl=1)

It‚Äôs awkward to talk to someone who doesn‚Äôt talk back yet, but the research is clear‚Äîthere‚Äôs a strong link between the number of words spoken to a child from birth and their later vocabulary skills. In the first few months, your baby is constantly listening to the intonation, rhythm, and patterns of your voice. Even though they can‚Äôt understand what you‚Äôre saying yet, their brain is laying the groundwork for acquiring language.

Some tips for talking to your baby:

![Baby sitting on a lap looking at a book by Lovevery](https://i0.wp.com/blog.lovevery.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/Grow-Newborns-Brain_Body-7.jpg?resize=1176%2C784&ssl=1)

* Try the reading position above‚Äîthey‚Äôre more likely to take an interest in the book if they can see your mouth and facial expressions. Also, this position helps to support awkward, slumpy little bodies with your legs.
* Go on a¬†[‚Äúhouse tour.‚Äù](https://blog.lovevery.com/child-development/why-a-simple-light-switch-is-better-for-your-baby-than-those-flashing-light-toys/)¬†When your baby is awake, facing forward in a wrap, if possible, or with their head turned to the side in a carrier, walk around your house and talk about what you‚Äôre seeing and experiencing. * Talk aloud to your baby about what you‚Äôre doing together‚Äî just narrate your day. During changing time, talk face to face.
* [Make eye contact](https://blog.lovevery.com/child-development/how-eye-contact-affects-your-babys-brain-development/)¬†and respond to their cues. If they start to coo or vocalize, talk back. ‚ÄúServe and return‚Äù communication is a well-studied element of a child‚Äôs brain development.
* Sing to your baby, even if you don‚Äôt think you have a great voice‚Äîyour baby will love it because it‚Äôs you. Babies tune into singing even more than talking.
* Talk in the slower, higher-pitched, sing-song voice that tends to come naturally when speaking to babies. Babies are pre-programmed to tune into higher tones, and learn more from the stretched-out vowels in ‚Äúbaby talk.‚Äù
* Tell your partner, your baby‚Äôs grandparents, and other caregivers how important talking is to your baby‚Äôs developing brain. Encourage them to find ways to talk to your baby, too‚Äîthe more words your baby hears, the easier their language acquisition can become later on.

### **Exploration of new sounds**

Around 4 weeks, your baby will likely start to show more interest in a variety of sounds and patterns. Help them build more lasting neural networks by exposing them to sounds from real life, rather than the pre-recorded sounds made by electronic toys. Experiment with sounds that are high-pitched, low-pitched, slow-paced, lively, soft, etc, introducing just one sound at a time.

When your baby is alert and the room is quiet, make simple sounds in their field of vision: tap on the glass, rattle your keys, snap your fingers. These everyday noises are routine for you, but they‚Äôre brand new (and therefore interesting) to your baby. Try to involve your baby as much as possible, talking about what you‚Äôre doing and what they‚Äôre seeing, even though they won‚Äôt understand what you‚Äôre saying quite yet.

Some sound-exploring ideas to get started:

* Tear or crumple up paper in front of your baby.
* Pour dry pasta into a pan, tap a spoon against a bowl, or make other common cooking noises.
* Turn a doorknob, ring a doorbell, and open or close the blinds or windows.
* Play and dance along to music.

As with other forms of stimulation, be sensitive to your baby‚Äôs reactions. If they‚Äôre unhappy, it‚Äôs time to take a break.
