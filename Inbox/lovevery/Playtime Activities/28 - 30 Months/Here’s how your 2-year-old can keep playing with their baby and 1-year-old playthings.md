### Spinning Rainbow

Now that your child is able to identify more colors, the [Spinning Rainbow](https://lovevery.com/products/the-play-kits-the-senser) from The Senser Play Kit provides a fun, randomized way of practicing color matching. Here are a few ways to use it:

* Start by teaching or reviewing the five colors of the Spinning Rainbow: red, orange, yellow, green, and blue. * Go on a color hunt. Start by spinning the plaything; when it stops, the color on top is the scavenger hunt color. Look around your home for objects that match the color, and bring them back to compare.
* Ask your child to spin the Spinning Rainbow as fast as they can, then stop it with their hand‚Äîwhen it stops, ask them to name the color their hand is on.

### Wooden Stacking Pegboard

The [Wooden Stacking Pegboard](https://lovevery.com/products/the-play-kits-the-adventurer) from The Adventurer Play Kit can grow alongside your child in a wide variety of ways, from counting to color-matching to sensory activities:

* Counting while deliberately pointing to each object in order may be a challenge for now, but it helps reinforce the number‚Äôs meaning for your child. This is called one-to-one correspondence, and the pegboard is an ideal way to practice: Give your child a handful of pegs‚Äîfive or so‚Äîand ask them to place the pegs, one at a time, onto the board as you both count. If they count too fast or mix up the numbers, start over and go more slowly.
* Try placing three pegs on the board and ask your child to count them. Next, remove one of the pegs and put it behind your back. Ask your child how many are left, then ask how many are in your hand. If your child is into it, keep going slowly, adding more to the board and varying the number of pegs you hide.
* If your child can sort the pegs into color groups, invite them to build towers of single colors. To further extend their color play, you can encourage them to make a tower with all four colors. Or, make a tower with only three colors and ask your child which one is missing.
