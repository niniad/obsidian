## Make a frame game

Create a fun game that teaches facial recognition and object permanence. All you need are some simple household items and photos of family and friends:

**Materials:**

1. Several wipe containers or cardboard and scissors
2. Large piece of cardboard
3. Glue
4. Photographs of family and friends

**Description:**

1. Collect several lids from wipe containers. Or, use cardboard to make flap-covered frames.
2. Glue three sides of each lid base or frame to a large piece of cardboard.
3. When the glue is dry, slide a picture through the open side of each frame.
4. Show your baby how to open a flap. Narrate with enthusiasm, ‚ÄúWho‚Äôs there? Oh! It‚Äôs Daddy!‚Äù
5. Let your baby try. Give them time to explore and discover how to open the flaps.
6. Change the pictures regularly to keep the surprises coming üôÇ

Please be sure to supervise your baby at all times during this activity and put the game safely away when play is done.

## Let it roll!

Give your baby an early science lesson by rolling an object down a ramp. Place a tower of [blocks](https://lovevery.com/products/the-block-set) at the bottom for a dramatic end.

1. Place a Play Kit or other box on the floor and open up the top to create a ramp.
2. [Stack](https://blog.lovevery.com/skills-stages/stacking/) three blocks on the tuck-in flap at the bottom of the ramp.
3. Hold a tube on its side at the top of the ramp, then release it so it rolls down and knocks down the blocks.
4. Stack the blocks and do it again. This time, you can help your baby send the tube down the ramp. You can try sitting your child inside the box to do this ‚ù§Ô∏è

### When will your baby crawl?

Here are some typical age ranges for the progression toward crawling, but keep in mind that the process is different for every baby.

1. Scooting backward on belly: typically 7 to 8 months
2. Scooting forward on belly by pulling with arms and pushing with legs: typically 8¬†to 9¬Ω months
3. Crawling with belly off the floor: typically 6 to 10 months

## Validate your baby‚Äôs signals

Communication at 10 months doesn‚Äôt involve words or sometimes even sounds. It can come in the form of a simple glance or gesture.

1. Sit with your baby in front of a clear tube and balls.
2. Drop a ball into the tube, then pause to see if your baby tries to communicate with you.
3. If they make eye contact, wave their hands, or utter a simple vocalization such as a coo, be sure to respond promptly.

An immediate, warm, and encouraging response tells your baby that their thoughts and feelings are important and lays the foundation for a relationship built on trust ‚ù§Ô∏è

## Crawlers take note

Challenge your crawler to follow a trail of sticky notes, picking them up one at a time. They‚Äôll use their core muscles to balance and build fine motor skills as they retrieve the notes.

1. Place a trail of multi-colored sticky notes 6 to 8 inches apart on the floor.
2. Sit your baby at one end of the trail.
3. Model how to pick up a sticky note, then encourage your baby to do the same.
4. Sit at the other end of the trail and call their name to encourage them to move in your direction.
5. As their skills and stamina increase, lengthen the trail with more sticky notes and veer it around a kitchen island or coffee table ‚ù§Ô∏è

Please supervise your baby at all times during this activity and ensure they do not mouth the sticky notes. Small items like these could become choking hazards. Put them safely away when play is done.

2. Get them interested by saying something like, ‚ÄúOh, I hear a bird! [Listen.] Do you hear that tweet?‚Äù
3. Use gestures and an excited tone to inspire the same enthusiasm in your baby üôÇ
