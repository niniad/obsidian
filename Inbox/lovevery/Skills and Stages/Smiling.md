## **When do babies start smiling?**

When they are 5 to 8 weeks old, you may start to see your baby‚Äôs first smiles. Some researchers suggest that these may be early social smiles, but most believe this kind of facial movement is reflexive.

By the time your baby is 2 to 4 months old, they may smile briefly as a social gesture. Social smiles last longer and are generally in response to something‚Äîlike your touch, your voice, or your smile. With a genuine social smile, your baby‚Äôs eyes may light up and they may even wiggle and squeal.

### **Reflex smiling and smirking**

Before your baby‚Äôs first social smile, you may notice fleeting expressions that resemble smiles. In a newborn, this type of facial expression may look like a smirk, with one or both corners of their mouth slightly raised. These reflex smiles may occur during naps or after feedings.

Fleeting expressions that resemble smiles are most likely reflexive facial movements.

### **Why do babies smile in their sleep?**

The adorable smiles your newborn makes in their sleep aren‚Äôt actually caused by sweet dreams‚Äîthose are reflexive smiles, too. During the phase of deeper sleep known as Rapid Eye Movement (REM) sleep, babies may twitch, make reflexive facial expressions, and even breathe differently. Researchers believe that these smile-like expressions may be your baby‚Äôs nervous system and facial muscles learning to coordinate with one another.

### **Social smiling**

Your baby‚Äôs intentional smiles will likely look different from their earlier, reflexive smiles. Social smiles last longer, and you might notice your baby‚Äôs mouth turns up and their eyes close a bit. These social smiles are among your baby‚Äôs first communications with you. They‚Äôre called Duchenne smiles, after the 19th century researcher who mapped the muscles responsible for facial expressions. Your baby may smile when you smile, make eye contact, or talk to them.

One of the reasons social smiling develops at this age is because your baby‚Äôs vision is maturing. At around 2 months of age, your baby‚Äôs eyes can focus on faces more than they could before. Your baby‚Äôs brain is also able to interpret people‚Äôs facial features better than even a month ago, which improves their ability to understand expressions.

Smiling at your baby doesn‚Äôt just benefit their developing social skills‚Äîit prompts a positive emotional response in you as well. Studies show that parents have a strong emotional and physiological reaction to their smiling babies. Seeing your smiling baby activates the part of your brain that controls feel-good emotions.

## **How do I encourage my baby to smile?**

Once you experience those first social smiles from your baby, you won‚Äôt be able to get enough of them. There are a few things you can do to encourage those sweet smiles:

**Speak to your baby**   
Research shows that the first social smiles often happen in response to [parentese](https://blog.lovevery.com/child-development/parentese-improves-childrens-vocabulary/)‚Äîthat gentle, lilting, sing-song voice that seems to come so naturally around babies üôÇ Combining speech and a gentle touch will help engage their attention. It‚Äôs in these moments of mutual gaze and communication that the earliest smiles most often emerge.

**RELATED: [Why your face is your baby‚Äôs best ‚Äútoy‚Äù](https://blog.lovevery.com/child-development/why-your-face-is-your-babys-best-toy/)**

**Connect face-to-face**  
Engage in face-to-face interactions with your baby when they‚Äôre most alert, like right after a nap or a meal. You might try gently singing or stroking their belly as you talk to them. During these sessions, go slow and [pause to give your baby a chance to respond](https://blog.lovevery.com/child-development/what-is-serve-and-return-and-why-is-it-so-important/). You may get the smile you‚Äôre hoping for, or you might get eye contact with excited movement of the hands and feet. These sessions may only last for one to two minutes before your baby signals they need a break by squirming or looking away‚Äîand that‚Äôs okay.

**Get social during daily activities**  
Use bathtime, diaper changes, and feedings to elicit social smiling. For instance, sing your little one a song about what you‚Äôre doing, such as, ‚ÄúI‚Äôm washing your toes, toes, toes, and now your belly, belly, belly.‚Äù If this prompts your baby to glance at you, pause and give them a big smile before you continue.

**Smile and make eye contact**  
Eye contact and nonverbal communication through smiles and cuddles help foster a strong bond between you and your baby, while supporting their development. Researchers find that social signals like [eye contact](https://blog.lovevery.com/child-development/the-power-of-eye-contact-with-your-baby/) and smiling are associated with improved social learning in babies. As your baby matures, they learn that smiling can help them keep their caregivers‚Äô attention. Even in the earliest months, they‚Äôre learning about subtle social interaction.

**Keep interactions short**¬†  
Most very young babies enjoy shorter social interactions rather than long periods of play. Try smiling at your baby, having cooing ‚Äúconversations,‚Äù running your fingers lightly across their tummy, or gently tapping their feet together while you softly sing.

## **When should I worry about my baby not smiling?**

If you haven‚Äôt seen signs of intentional smiles by 4 months of age, it‚Äôs a good idea to talk to your child‚Äôs pediatrician. Keep in mind that each baby has their own unique personality, even at just a few months old. Some babies smile more often than others simply by temperament. Keep connecting with animated face-to-face time, imitate your baby‚Äôs sounds and expressions, and enjoy each other ‚ù§Ô∏è

## **What comes after smiling for babies?**

Smiling is just the beginning of your baby‚Äôs social and communication skills. After that first social smile, your baby will continue to learn how to engage with you and others through nonverbal interactions. Here are a few developments to look forward to as your baby grows:

* **2 to 6 months:** Your baby may smile or show signs of recognition when they see a familiar person.
* **3 to 4 months:** Your baby may wiggle, make sounds, and show excitement when you engage with them~~.~~
* **5 to 9 months:** You‚Äôll recognize your baby‚Äôs emotions from their facial expressions‚Äîfor example, you may see their eyebrows go up when they‚Äôre surprised or excited, or their forehead furrow when they‚Äôre distressed or frustrated.

As your baby grows, they‚Äôll learn to combine those social smiles with [coos](https://blog.lovevery.com/child-development/4-ways-to-encourage-your-babys-first-coos/), laughs and eventually words. You and other caregivers can play a key role here: Your baby will be more motivated to keep interacting if they get a consistent response from you. Engaging with your baby‚Äôs early smiles and sounds helps support their social development.
