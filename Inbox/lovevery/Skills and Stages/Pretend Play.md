## **What is pretend play?**

Pretend play is a broad category that includes many different kinds of representational play. As they grow, your child will likely imitate things they‚Äôve experienced in real life, take on different roles, and act out imaginary scenarios. Pretend play is real evidence of your child‚Äôs growing cognitive skills, symbolic understanding, and imagination.

## **When should my child start doing pretend play?**

You may see your child first begin to engage in pretend play around 18 to 24 months. Perhaps they hold a block to their ear and pretend to use it as a phone, or ‚Äúfeed‚Äù a doll using a peg for a baby bottle. This is a form of symbolic play called **object substitution**, and it‚Äôs the earliest stage of pretend play.

Before pretend play, your child may engage in what experts call **functional play**. This typically happens around 13 to 18 months and involves imitating or repeating things your child has seen in real life, using playthings according to their intended purpose. Examples of functional play include pushing a toy car across the floor, using a toy phone as a real phone, and making a Bunny ‚Äúhop‚Äù into the Felt Burrow. Functional play is a sign of your toddler‚Äôs growing memory‚Äîthey can remember something they saw or something you did, and reenact it. Research suggests this type of play may serve as a foundation for the development of pretend play.

Pretend play is different from functional play. To pretend, your child must think symbolically, using objects or playthings to represent something entirely different‚Äîlike a peg for a baby bottle. This represents a significant cognitive shift in your child‚Äôs development. They‚Äôre no longer simply imitating actions they‚Äôve seen you do or using an object for its intended purpose. They‚Äôve entered a new stage of symbolic play.

## **What are the different types and stages of pretend play?**

As your child‚Äôs cognitive and language skills grow, they‚Äôll begin to explore different types of pretend play. These don‚Äôt necessarily develop in a progression of stages. Instead, think of them as variations of pretend play that call on different skills.

### **Symbolic play**: starts around 18 to 24 months

![Child playing with the Organic Cotton Baby Doll and the Fuzzy Bug Shrub from Lovevery](https://i0.wp.com/blog.lovevery.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/SymbolicPlay_C.jpg?resize=1024%2C1024&ssl=1)

When your toddler cares for dolls, they‚Äôre engaging in symbolic play that reenacts familiar experiences. In photo: Soft Cotton Baby Doll from [The Thinker Play Kit](https://lovevery.com/products/the-play-kits-the-thinker)

In this type of pretend play, your child may use one object to symbolically represent another, also called object substitution. They may pretend to use a banana as a phone, or slurp imaginary soup from a stick they‚Äôre pretending is a spoon.

At first, your child‚Äôs symbolic play scenarios will be simple‚Äîjust one or two steps focused on a single plaything‚Äîand will most often reenact their own lived experiences. When your toddler puts their baby doll to bed, gives it a bath, or brushes its hair, that shows that they remember a sequence of events in a bedtime routine. When they use a spoon to pretend they‚Äôre mixing ingredients for pancakes, your toddler is demonstrating that they know how things work. They can use objects the right way while pretending to complete a task.

### **Dramatic play**: starts around 2.5 to 3 years

Dramatic play is the type of pretend play you might remember from your own childhood, where children act out different play scenarios. At first, this type of pretend play focuses on events your child has experienced in real life. They may reenact these scenarios with dolls, figures, or stuffed animals. For example, your child might act out ‚Äúgoing to Grandma‚Äôs house‚Äù with their favorite stuffed animals, each animal representing a family member. As they grow, your child will start to use their imagination, working memory, and social skills to create more complex situations to act out.

### **Role play**: starts around 3 years

Around 3 years of age, your child‚Äôs pretend play may involve taking on roles that are familiar to them‚ÄîMama, Daddy, or even a character from a favorite show. You may see them pretend to be an animal or gather props for a play scenario they have in mind‚Äîa block may represent a phone or a doll might represent you ‚ù§Ô∏è Children this age like to try out roles that let them experiment with a feeling of power or importance, like a king, teacher, or firefighter.

By age 4, you may see your child give dolls, figures, or stuffed animals characters to play instead of taking on a particular role themselves. When they play ‚Äúput the baby to sleep‚Äù with a doll or stuffed animal, they may give the doll a voice and personality as the character in their play.

### **Imaginary objects**: starts around 3 to 4 years

As your child approaches their preschool years, they may begin to include another feature in their pretend play repertoire‚Äîimaginary objects. While their earlier pretend play may have involved props like toy dishes or tools, now your child may have the ability to create a pretend scenario that is completely imaginary. You may see them drink from an invisible cup or stir an invisible pot of soup.

Your child may begin to join with peers in their dramatic play scenes as early as 3 to 4 years of age, though this may not be [cooperative play](https://blog.lovevery.com/child-development/cooperative-play-how-young-children-build-friendships/) at first. ### **Fantasy play**: starts around 4 years

Around 4 years of age, your child‚Äôs pretend play may expand into the complex and imaginative world of **fantasy play.** During this stage, your child may act out not only what¬† they have lived and seen, but also animals, people, and places they have not yet experienced.

Fantasy play scenarios may involve situations that are completely created within your child‚Äôs imagination, with little or no reflection of reality. For example, your child may pretend to be an alien on a distant planet or a mermaid living under the sea. This type of¬†pretend play reflects your child‚Äôs growing understanding of abstract ideas. It also demonstrates their ability to draw upon prior knowledge. They haven‚Äôt actually seen a distant planet, but perhaps you read them a book about it. They‚Äôve never taken a ride on a shark or a unicorn, but they may have watched a show that referenced those animals.

### **Imaginary friends**: starts around 4 to 5 years

The preschool years are a time when many children invent an imaginary friend. Not all children engage in this type of play, but research suggests that one- to two-thirds of children have an imaginary friend at some point during childhood. Some play with this imaginary friend for months or even years. This is healthy example of active, elaborate pretend play.

## **Why is pretend play important in early childhood?**

Pretend play supports the development of a variety of skills, including social skills, problem solving, and language development. These are just a few of the ways your child benefits from exploring different types of pretend play:

**Understanding other people‚Äôs feelings:** Research suggests that pretend play is associated with a greater ability to understand other people‚Äôs feelings and perspectives. To¬†play along with a pretend scenario, your child must understand that one object or action represents something else‚Äîlike a block representing a phone. This ability to imagine how one thing can represent another also helps your child imagine what another person is experiencing or feeling.

**Social skills:** As preschool-age children listen to one another and negotiate the ‚Äúrules‚Äù of a pretend play scenario, they learn about how others think and feel. By playing ‚Äúhouse‚Äù or ‚Äúgrocery store,‚Äù they begin to collaborate with others and learn how to negotiate and take turns.

**Problem-solving practice:**Pretend play, especially with peers, means coming up with rules, establishing roles, and working toward goals. For example, when your child and their friends play superheroes, they need to agree on who plays each role, and what‚Äôs considered ‚Äúacceptable‚Äù superhero behavior: Can the hero have a gadget? A cape? What superpowers do they have? All these decisions involve problem-solving, both individually and as a group.

Not surprisingly, research has shown a link between pretend play and children‚Äôs problem-solving skills. Pretend play encourages children to ‚Äúthink outside the box,‚Äù which experts call divergent thinking, and use language in innovative ways, which is known as semantic problem solving.

In fact, research suggests pretend play and problem solving may have a reciprocal effect. As children‚Äôs problem-solving skills are put to work through pretend play, pretend play may also give their problem-solving skills a boost, making them more creative, out-of-the-box thinkers.

**Language skills:** Your child may learn new words from play partners or from you while engaging in pretend play. Parents playing pretend tend to do more conversational turn-taking, make more sound effects, and ask more question‚Äîall of which stimulate language development.

On a deeper level, your child‚Äôs ability to participate in pretend play indicates their brain can grasp symbolic representation, or that one object can represent another. This same symbolic understanding is part of how your child learns language:¬† Letters are symbols representing sounds, and words are symbols used to represent objects or concepts.

While the link between pretend play and language development is complex, research indicates they are clearly related. Your child‚Äôs ability to play pretend may develop alongside their language skills, or one may influence the other. Either way, supporting pretend play is an effective and fun way to encourage your child‚Äôs language development.

**Emotional processing:** Pretend play may help your child process both positive and challenging emotional experiences. When they‚Äôre acting out a particular scenario, children have an opportunity to break down an experience, make their own rules, and try out alternative endings. Research on pretend play shows that children often use it as a way to play out themes about aggression, illness, or accidents. This gives them¬† the opportunity to feel powerful in a world where they have little control and sometimes feel overwhelmed. Pretending to be an important figure in their life can be incredibly empowering for your child‚Äîtaking the role of caregiver and bandaging up a stuffed bunny feels pretty cathartic after getting a skinned knee.
