## **When do babies start laughing and giggling?**

Your baby will likely giggle for the first time somewhere between 3 and 4 months of age. Laughing happens when babies combine a vocalization with a social smile. Your baby‚Äôs first laugh may be short and quiet. But over time, it will develop into full belly laughter ‚ù§Ô∏è

At first, it won‚Äôt be something funny that causes your baby to laugh‚Äîthey don‚Äôt experience humor the way adults do. Instead, they‚Äôll giggle because of something new or unexpected, or in reaction to you.

Your baby‚Äôs first laugh will combine a social smile with a short, quiet vocalization.

## **When do babies become ticklish?**

By around 6 months of age, your baby will respond to tickles with giggles and laughter.

As young as 4 months, their sense of touch is sensitive enough to identify when someone touches their foot or their belly, but they may not react as though they‚Äôre being tickled. Why doesn‚Äôt a 4-month-old show signs of being ticklish, even though they‚Äôre aware of a light, tickling touch? Being tickled isn‚Äôt just a sensory experience‚Äîit‚Äôs a form of social interaction, too. Your baby needs enough social awareness to understand the nonverbal cues that come with tickling, such as smiling, laughing, anticipation, and [eye contact](https://blog.lovevery.com/child-development/the-power-of-eye-contact-with-your-baby/). They also have to learn how to connect the physical sensation with the person doing the tickling. So while your 4-month-old can feel your foot stroke or belly rub, they simply haven‚Äôt pieced together all the sensory components to giggle at a tickle‚Ä¶yet.

## **How can I encourage my baby to laugh?**

To encourage that first giggle, try playing peekaboo. When your baby is very young, they don‚Äôt understand that you‚Äôre still there when they can‚Äôt see you. ### **Why is my baby laughing in their sleep?**

If you‚Äôve seen your baby laughing in their sleep, you can probably tell it‚Äôs not a social laugh but more of an involuntary action. Researchers aren‚Äôt completely certain why babies sometimes laugh in their sleep, but it may be related to their sleep cycle.

Like adults, newborns and older babies experience cycles of active sleep‚Äîalso called Rapid Eye Movement or REM sleep‚Äîand quiet sleep, which is also called Non-Rapid Eye Movement or NREM sleep. But your baby can still move their muscles during REM sleep, while an adult can‚Äôt. Scientists suggest this may be why babies can laugh, smile, or twitch during their sleep.

## **Why is my baby not laughing?**

Your baby may laugh easily, or not so easily, depending on their personal characteristics. Every baby has their own unique personality and temperament.

If your baby is not yet laughing at 4 or 5 months of age, look for other signs of interaction. Do they vocalize and connect with you in other ways? Do they smile, initiate eye contact, and seem to enjoy playing with you? If so, there‚Äôs likely no cause for concern. Keep engaging in back-and-forth ‚Äúconversations,‚Äù imitate your baby‚Äôs sounds and expressions, and enjoy lots of face-to-face time.

If your baby hasn‚Äôt laughed by 9 months of age, experts suggest talking with your pediatrician.

Starting between 12 and 18 months, your toddler may begin to notice what is normal and what is unexpected or out of the ordinary, and find humor in contradictions. You can encourage their laughter by doing something silly. For example, pretend to drink from their bottle or cup, or try to balance a ball on your head and let it fall.

As your child gets older, what they find funny changes along with their growing understanding of the world. Their sense of humor gives you a peek into the ideas they understand. For example, in toddlerhood, your child begins to know that many items have a certain place or use, and they often find ‚Äúout of place‚Äù things funny. For example, they know that mittens go on your hands and socks on your feet. If you try to put a mitten on your foot or one of their shoes on their head, your toddler will probably find it hilarious. Later, as their language skills develop, your child will start to see the humor in wordplay or puns.

Humor may support an important aspect of brain development because it engages both sides of the brain. The left side lights up when your child thinks of jokes, while the right side of their brain is activated by the social-emotional response to humor‚Äîlaughing and appreciating the joke.

Neuroscientists believe that hearing another person laugh triggers mirror neurons that make the listener feel like they‚Äôre laughing themselves. When you see a person laugh, mirror neurons fire that prompt you to imitate that action yourself. This is why laughter is contagious ‚ù§Ô∏è These same mirror neurons are the ones that prompt you to show empathy toward someone.
